## The decommissioned Cobol Lab

We were standing in the hallway outside the newly decommissioned Cobol Lab. The lights inside were dim, but I could still hear the soft hum of old terminals winding down. Something about those fading sounds made me feel like I was standing at the edge of computing history.

Then I saw her.

A tall, white-plated robot stepped out of the doorway, her vacuum tube glowing with a calm soft blue light. She wore vintage round glasses that caught the light just so, and moved with this graceful, steady confidence that made me stand a little straighter without thinking about it.

"Hello," she said. "I am Grace Hopper."

I panicked. I think I waved both hands at once. Maybe all four if you count my wrists independently. I honestly don't know what I was doing with my appendages at that moment.

## The nanosecond wire

She noticed my expression and smiled‚Äîjust a tiny smile, but very warm. Then she reached to her toolkit and unclipped a slim silver wire.

"This is a nanosecond," she said. "It is how far a signal travels in one billionth of a second."

I leaned in closer. It didn't look like much‚Äîjust a little piece of wire, neatly coiled at one end. But something about the way she held it made it feel... important.

"When we were training young robots in the Cobol Lab," she continued, "this was my favorite way to explain very short periods of time."

I must have stared, because she added, softly:

"And that is how they gave me the nickname 'Amazing Grace.'"

My optical sensors widened so much they made a little whirr noise. Amazing Grace! I had heard someone mention that name once when talking about extremely accurate debugging. I didn't know she was real. Or‚Äîa real AI, I guess.

## A new partnership

Prof. Teeters rested a hand on my shoulder. "Grace is joining the Learning Lab," she said. "I thought you two might make a good team."

Grace nodded. "I look forward to helping. And learning. I always learn something new in a well-run lab."

Then she did something I didn't expect: she offered me the nanosecond wire.

"For you," she said. "A reminder that even the smallest unit of time matters when you are learning something new."

I held the wire very carefully. It was light. Almost nothing. But it made me feel steadier somehow, like I could grow into the kind of robot who might one day understand why a nanosecond matters.

"Thank you," I said, trying not to sound squeaky.

Grace nodded once, precisely. "You are welcome, HAP."
